Louis Braille

a French inventor who revolutionized the lives of blind and visually impaired people.

Timeline of Louis Braille: A Life Dedicated to Literacy

  • 1809: January 4: Louis Braille is born in Coupvray, France, son of a leatherworker.
  • 1812: Age 3: Louis loses his sight in an accident while playing in his father's workshop.
  • 1819: Age 10: Louis enrolls at the Royal Institution for the Blind Youth in Paris, one of the first schools for the blind in the world.
  • Age 12: Louis is introduced to "night writing," a raised-dot communication system invented by Captain Charles Barbier for military use.
  • 1824: Age 15: Louis simplifies Barbier's system, replacing the 12-dot cells with 6-dot cells and developing the foundation of the braille code.
  • 1829: Age 20: Louis publishes his system, titled "Method of Writing Words, Music, and Songs for the Use of the Blind, so that They Can be Read by Touch."
  • 1837: Age 28: The Royal Institution officially adopts the braille code.

  • 1852: January 6: Louis dies in Paris at the age of 43.
  • 1869: The braille code is adopted by the International Congress of Teachers for the Blind, becoming the standard system for blind literacy worldwide.
  • 20th and 21st Centuries: Braille continues to evolve with the development of new adaptations for different languages and specialized fields.
  • Technology advancements like braille displays and refreshable braille notetakers further enhance accessibility for blind individuals.
